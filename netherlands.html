Travel to the Netherlands & First Impressions

My 29-hour journey and initial cultural experiences

My adventure to the Netherlands started with a marathon 29-hour journey from Johannesburg. It was a first for everything: my first ever flight and my first train ride. I was travelling with Jan, and we were both wide-eyed the whole way, I don't think I slept at all that whole night.

Our first stop was Doha. Let me tell you, that airport is something else, it's just massive. The views from the plane were incredible, from mountains in the Middle East to the tiny islands along the coast near Qatar. Our final part of the flight over the Netherlands was a patchwork of unbelievably green farms.

After a long flight, we hit a snag: nearly two hours stuck in Amsterdam's border control. Once we finally got through, we had to tackle the Dutch train system. We were pretty lost and even missed a connecting train, but some really kind locals patiently helped us figure it out.

We finally made it to Eindhoven and found our home for the next 3 weeks: the Stayokay Hostel. The four of us South Africans are sharing one room with four beds. It is a bit small and crowded, and sharing a room with near-strangers felt weird at first.

The culture shock was immediate and surreal. Back in South Africa, life moves on four wheels; your own car is a necessity. But here, the rhythm of the city is set by bikes swarming through dedicated lanes, trams, trains, and buses. The streets have a steady flow of people, but everyone is sealed off in their own space, a quiet, detached feeling that is completely different from the open, chatty buzz of life back in South Africa.

Academic Experience

The first week at Fontys kicked off with intros and workshops. We got a tour of the ICT campus, which was way more teched-out than I'd imagined. Our main project is pretty cool; we have to design an IT solution for water quality issues back at Hartbeespoort Dam in South Africa which is in issue I am personally familiar with as I live about on a farm about 10 minutes away from Hartbeespoort town.

We jumped into the Immersive Tech module, got an intro to all sorts of tech, and had sessions on Intercultural Competencies that got me thinking about working in a global team. The Design Thinking workshop was a highlight, with a super hands-on session, tackling "how might we" questions and creating design challenges, which already helped our team come up with a concept for the solution. The week wrapped with group presentations, which was a nice way to see everyone's early ideas.

Most of our time was spent on research. As a team, we put together a deep-dive document, and I focused individually on the intro, background, problem statement, and objectives. For the individual research I looked into IoT and sensor networks.

Cultural & Social Activities

Outside of class, the week was packed. We even started kickboxing lessons, which turned out to be an awesome way to blow off steam and try something completely new. Monday was an icebreaker at a student bar, Tuesday was pool night, and Wednesday we explored the city after group work. I skipped Thursday's student night to recharge, but by Friday I was checking out the Afghan mosque and wandering around. Learning Style & Cultural Observations

The biggest cultural shift was the learning style. Fontys is all about independence, as the lecturers expect you to take initiative and collaborate, which is different from the more guided approach I'm used to. I really liked how interactive and discussion-based the classes were. But I'm not sure that it is a system that would work for me. It just seems like learning more complicated subjects like Calculus would be extremely difficult and chaotic.

The city itself moves differently too. Life here feels calm and organized. Everyone's minding their own business and usually have headphones on. It's a stark contrast to South Africa, where public spaces feel more social and alert. Even the 9 p.m. sunsets take getting used to!

Structured reflections on cultural adaptation and personal growth

Week 1 — Self-assessment (What? So what? Now what?)

What?

The first week is mostly about settling in and getting to know the project. We did a campus introduction, introduction to the Hartbeespoort Dam project brief, intercultural competencies, and a design thinking workshops. I also started my individual IoT research and the team in-depth research document. In the team, I mostly listen at first and then contribute structured points once I'm comfortable. The language barrier shows up sometimes, not everyone follows technical terms easily.

So what?

This shows me two things: clear, simple communication is key in a mixed team, and my natural style of wait, then speaking is useful for structure but can mean I miss early input moments. I notice that Fontys has a more independent style of teaching compared to what I'm used to; lecturers expect you to figure things out, not spoon feed every step, like we are back home. Emotionally, I'm excited but also tired after the travel, which affects how engaged I feel.

Now what?

  • Speak earlier in brainstorming (at least one idea or question in the first 5 minutes).
  • Build a short IoT cheat sheet (summary) so everyone understands the basics.
  • Reflect weekly on my energy and engagement so I don't fade into the background when I'm tired.

Week 2 — Self-assessment (STARR)

Situation:

Week 2 is full of activities: Aquality's demo with their sensor boat, Amsterdam excursion, Enversed VR visit, Waterschap in Tiel, as well as group work and workshops where we developed three concepts.

Task:

My task is to research and flesh out Concept 1: IoT sensor station and dashboard. I need to make sure that the concept is realistic, technically solid, and documented so the rest of the team understands it.

Action:

I dove into IoT sensor options and data flow, thinking about how to make it simple but useful. I also write up the details for the group document. During excursions, I pay attention to how real-world projects (Aquality, Waterschap) keep solutions practical and focused.

Result:

The 3 concepts comes together well, and my teammates rely on my input for the on concept 1 based of my research. But I realize I've spent too much time deep in details and not enough aligning my assumptions with the others, especially those focused on design or user experience.

Reflection:

This experience taught me that true project leadership involves two key roles: being the technical anchor and the team's translator. I am confident in my ability to go deep and structure complex work, but I now see that my weakness is in bringing everyone along on that journey. For next time, I am committed to making my explanations more accessible, leveraging visuals, and regularly ensuring my non-technical colleagues are aligned and engaged.

Week 3 — Self-assessment (John's Model of Structured Reflection)

Description:

Week 3 is about finalizing the project plan. As a team, we define scope, objectives, research questions, and risks. We create user profiles, pick one of the 3 concepts, and present our final project plan. I focused on the problem statement, community engagement, budget, and supporting the presentation. We also did a retrospective and 360 feedback session.

Feelings:

At the start of the week, I feel the pressure of deadlines. It's a lot of detail to pull together. When my teammates give positive feedback on our group structure and reliability, I feel more confident. Social events (student party, Feel Good markets, Die Nag) give me balance and let me destress.

Evaluation:

What went well: our group worked efficiently, we divided tasks fairly, and the final project plan looked solid. My writing and structuring skills helped the group move forward.

What didn't go so well: I stayed quiet too long during creative discussions, leaving the louder voices to set direction.

Analysis:

This shows me my comfort zone is in refining and organizing, not in throwing ideas out quickly. It's a strength because it ensures quality, but it limits my influence on creative direction.

Conclusion:

I learn that being effective in a team isn't just about doing your part well, it's also about timing and visibility. Waiting too long to speak means my ideas don't shape the conversation.

Action plan:

  • In future projects ideation sessions, I should share ideas and questions within the as soon as they pop into my head.
  • Keep building on my strength in structure and research but balance it by stepping into the creative flow earlier.
  • Use visuals or short summaries when presenting technical content so everyone stays engaged.

What I Didn't Know Before This Module

Immersive Technologies

Before the module, my understanding of VR and AR was mostly limited to gaming and entertainment. Through the Immersive Tech workshops and the excursion to Enversed VR Center, I learned how these technologies can be applied in broader contexts such as training, education, and team collaboration. I discovered their potential as tools for simulations, e.g., for training people to repair IoT stations.

User-Centered Design & Design Thinking

Although I had worked on technical projects before, I had not applied structured design thinking methods. The "How Might We" workshops, user profiling exercises, and design challenges taught me how to focus on stakeholders' needs rather than just technical feasibility. For example, creating user profiles of fishermen, tourists, and locals gave me a new lens to align solutions with real-world users.

Intercultural Competencies in Practice

I knew cultural differences existed, but the Intercultural Competency sessions showed me practical tools for navigating them. I learned frameworks for recognizing how communication styles, work habits, and cultural expectations influence group dynamics. For example, I became more aware of the Dutch style of direct communication and punctuality, compared to the more conversational and flexible style I was used to in South Africa.

Water Management Systems

I did not know how advanced Dutch water management systems are. The excursion to the Waterschap in Tiel showed me how technology is applied on a national scale to monitor and improve water quality. This broadened my perspective on how IoT and data collection could be integrated into environmental solutions.

Independent Learning Approaches

The Dutch academic system is very different from what I was used to in South Africa. At Fontys, lecturers gave us freedom to explore and expected initiative, which was sometimes uncomfortable but taught me to take more responsibility for my own learning. This independence was new to me, as I was more familiar with structured and guided teaching.

Skills I Had But Strengthened During This Module

Communication in Teams

While I already knew communication was important, this project pushed me to simplify technical concepts so that my non-technical teammates could follow my IoT research and solutions. I learned to use visuals, summaries, and step-by-step explanations.

Early Participation in Brainstorming

I recognized that my natural style was to listen first and contribute structured input later. Through feedback sessions I realized that waiting too long reduced my impact during ideation.

Leadership & Responsibility

While developing Concept 1 (IoT sensor stations with dashboards), I not only researched technical feasibility but also took responsibility for guiding the team on technical alignment. I learned that leadership is not only about expertise but about helping others connect with ideas and ensuring the group is aligned.

Project Structuring & Planning

I improved in translating ideas into structured outputs such as problem statements, research methods, objectives, risks, and budgets. This strengthened my ability to connect vision with practical execution.

Adaptability & Cultural Awareness

By living and studying in the Netherlands, I became more adaptable to cultural differences in daily life (transport, independence, social interactions). I improved my ability to adjust and function in unfamiliar systems while keeping an open mind.

Self-Reflection

Using structured reflection models (STARR, John's Model), I strengthened my ability to analyse experiences critically, identify my strengths and weaknesses, and create action plans for future improvement.
